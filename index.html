Projects

Project 1

🌸 Music of the Spheres

This project focuses on ambient music and space. The sketch generates a space scene with stars, galaxies, and planets. Each planet plays a different sound when the user hovers over it, and while the sound plays, sound waves and music notes appear. Clicking the mouse reshuffles the planets and regenerates the galaxy, so each interaction feels different. I was inspired by the idea of the “music of the spheres,” where planets and stars are imagined to move in harmony like a musical system. I also drew inspiration from ambient and generative art, where sound and visuals can evolve and respond to interaction, that overall creates a calm, immersive environment that changes each time it is experienced.

Project 2

🌸 Concept 🌸 Final

This project is inspired by the board game “the Game of Life,” but it is a more open-ended version. I wanted to show the less fixed version of life, and have it be more messy and less predictable. By making decisions and clicking through different milestones/thought processes of life, the user can see how expectations, purpose, and identity are involved. I also want this to almost redefine the rules of life in a way, so there will be errors and surprises along the way. With this, I wanted to invoke a feeling of contemplation and uncertainty and question the goal of life. The idea of ‘winning’ becomes unclear here, for it kind of goes into the illusion of control, or social life scripts, and choice/identity anxiety.

Project 3

🌸 Final 🌸 Concept

This project explores the everyday objects I use as part of a hidden global system of production and consumption. By visualizing the items we use daily as repetitive things, it shows how personal belongings are connected to mass manufacturing, distant labor, and unseen supply chains, which questions our familiarity, ownership, and where objects actually come from. I was inspired by Jenny Odell’s work, in how she used her personal items to reflect on overconsumption and how things are made. I also liked how she did this in a minimal way, which I tried to do as well, so that the focus is on the amount of objects being displayed, as well as what they are made out of. This project is meant to be reflective, as I have been reflecting on my own consumption, and I hope others reflect on overconsumption and being smarter with their purchases as well, for it has a bigger impact than we realize.

Vibe Coding

🌸 Exercise 1: I wanted to make a website that looks different every time you visit or refresh it. Starting with only that idea, I used Claude.AI to generate a simple animated site, then gradually shaped it into a playable game. The project evolved quickly through more specific prompts, which showed me how AI can turn a vague concept into an engaging, interactive experience. 🌸 Exercise 2: I created an interactive "map of maps" website that critiques how different visualization modes distort our understanding of digital content. It includes a space where data is compared in a timeline and bubble chart, which then explains the distortions of each. Every interface choice is a deliberate distortion that enables certain understandings while obscuring others. 🌸 Exercise 3: I was thinking about how the internet/”web” actually functions like a web. This game is now fully centered on the idea of navigating a living web, where the player moves along interconnected strands, hopping from node to node like they’re inside the internet’s network. It turns the internet’s “web” metaphor into something you can literally move through. 🌸 Exercise 4: “bluey” is a pastel-blue, friendly personality quiz that asks whimsical questions like favorite clouds or snacks. Behind the scenes, it tracks choices, cursor movement, and timing to generate playful “results.” Users may feel safe and charmingly engaged, but their behavior is subtly recorded and interpreted. Inspired by Lauren McCarthy, this mirrors her work exposing how everyday interactions like fun quizzes, apps, or interfaces quietly collect and shape personal data. The soft, cheerful aesthetic disguises the surveillance underneath, making the experience both sweet and eerily revealing. 🌸 Exercise 5: I was inspired by Yaa Addae and the idea that we "grew up online." That is such an interesting concept to me, and although I know this I feel like I never fully realized/think about how this impacts our lives in positive and negative ways. With this website though, I wanted to focus on the positives and show how much we have grown and been shaped by the internet, especially during years like covid. 🌸 Exercise 7: I read the algorithmic policing article and was shocked by some of the details surrounding how prevalent it was and how much is actually biased or affected by this. Although I understand the weight of this, I wanted to visualize it in a way that showed some of the extent to which this works as well as how it frames people and groups into statistics/data. Also, I wanted the users specifically to have to navigate through all of this monitoring and data to almost feel how wrong it is. 🌸 Exercise 8: I was inspired by Olivia Jack’s “maps for getting lost.” I thought the idea of these generative and deconstructed maps is very thought provoking, for it challenges the expectation that maps are meant to orient us. But I’ve learned that maps are powerful in the way that they provide information, so I wanted to combine that idea with Olivia Jack’s more abstract maps to show how maps being displayed in different ways affects how we read them. 🌸 Edited: I mainly just made the font sizes bigger and changed the color. Thoughts

Vibecoding was such an interesting experience for me, for not only did it show me the capabilities of code, and even AI, especially with project ideation or figuring out certain features. But also, it showed me how AI is not the only thing you need when making art. Although using AI to immediately create something was pretty easy and fast, which would seem convenient, it actually showed me a lot of its flaws. Like, how it wouldn’t listen to what I tell it to create, or it doesn’t work, or how it is limited in its ability to create creative, and especially accurate things. It also taught me a lot about how to be precise with what I want to create. Whether that was through prompting, or it doing something completely different than what I had envisioned, this showed me how it is important to have a vision or plan in the end so that the work makes sense and has meaning. I will say, vibecoding was a fun and frustrating experience, for while I got to explore different styles and ideas fairly quickly, it wasn’t always easy to get it to do what it thinks its doing. Sometimes, I would run out of credits while trying to get it to do a function that it already thought was working. Or, most of the projects I made with this seemed to lack a lot of depth or true meaning, which is something I already struggle with in my work, making it personal enough. So, I thought it was a cool way to introduce me to coding, but also show me that good work will come from myself and others that truly put in the effort to make something meaningful and cohesive.
